Background
What I was working on, and who for

I was placed at Baker Hughes through Capgemini as a UX designer on their internal asset management platform. Baker Hughes manages industrial equipment — turbines, compressors, drilling tools, sensors — across offshore platforms and onshore facilities globally. The platform I was working on is what field engineers use to register new equipment, track health and maintenance history, and raise service requests.

The engineers using this weren't casual users. They knew their equipment inside out. What they didn't have was a tool that matched how they actually worked. And in a context where a miscategorised asset or a missed commissioning date can mean a compliance failure, or a missed service on critical machinery, that gap mattered.

Requirements came from the stakeholder side. My job wasn't to define what data the system needed to capture — that was already decided. My job was to figure out how a real person, probably under time pressure on an offshore platform, could do that accurately without needing to think about the tool.

The Problem
The forms were technically correct. They just didn't work.

The existing flow had everything it needed from a data perspective. All the fields were there. The problem was structure — or the lack of it.

Asset registration involved 40+ fields in a single unbroken scroll. Mandatory and optional fields looked identical. There was no progress indicator, no grouping, no sense of how far through the task you were. Engineers were either abandoning halfway through, or submitting with gaps — particularly on fields like serial numbers and commissioning dates that were required for compliance but weren't visually distinct from anything else on the page.

The bigger issue was what happened when something went wrong. Errors only surfaced at submission, and the messages were generic. "This field is required." That's not useful when you've just filled in 40 fields and have no idea which one failed. For Class A critical assets, this meant compliance issues weren't caught until an audit flagged them weeks later.

And then there was the handoff problem. Designs were being passed to developers as flat screens with no annotations. Developers were making their own calls on validation logic, error states, conditional field rules — and what came out the other end often wasn't what was designed. The gap between the prototype and the build was where the user experience got lost.

Screen 1 of 5 — Dashboard
Starting with the overview

The dashboard is the first screen engineers see. It needed to give them a quick read on the state of their asset fleet without requiring any interaction. Four KPI cards at the top — total, operational, attention, critical — give the high-level picture immediately. The asset cards below show individual equipment with health bars so the state of each asset is readable at a glance.

Filter chips let engineers narrow by status or equipment type without navigating away. The sidebar stays consistent across every screen. The dashboard is intentionally passive — it's a read screen, not an action screen. That separation was a deliberate choice to keep the information hierarchy clear.

Design note

The dashboard is read-only by design. Engineers land here to understand the state of their fleet, not to take action immediately. Keeping it passive meant the information hierarchy stayed clear — nothing competing for attention with the status data.

Screen 2 of 5 — Inventory Table
Giving engineers their full list

The inventory table is where engineers manage their complete asset register. All 248 assets, filterable by status, type, or location. A specific frustration with the previous tool was that engineers knew an asset ID but couldn't find it quickly — search was buried, there were no filters, and sort order was arbitrary.

I added sortable columns, persistent filter chips, and inline status badges with health bars so engineers can triage by urgency without opening each record. Overdue maintenance shows directly in the "Next Due" column, colour-coded, so nothing important is hidden behind a click.

Screen 3 of 5 — Asset Registration Form
The form that caused the most problems — and needed the most work

This was the core of the project. Asset registration had the highest abandonment rate and the highest error rate. The previous form was one long unbroken page — 40+ fields visible at once, no grouping, no indication of which fields were mandatory versus optional, no validation until you hit submit.

I broke it into three named sections: Classification, Asset Details, and Location & Assignment. A progress sidebar on the right shows which step you're on and what's left. The sidebar also auto-generates an Asset ID and flags incomplete fields before submission — so engineers know what to fix before they even try.

The validation banner shows as soon as an engineer tabs out of a required field. The error on Serial Number says: "Required for Class A assets." Not "Required." Specific to what they're registering. Engineers know their equipment — they just need the system to explain what it needs and why.

Screen 4 of 5 — Maintenance Request Form
Raising a request without losing context

The maintenance request form had a specific problem: engineers would navigate to it, start filling it in, and then realise they needed to check something about the asset — health score, last service date, whether it was running or shut down. In the old flow, that meant leaving the form and losing progress.

The sidebar on this screen shows the asset context persistently throughout — health bar, asset ID, current status, and last three service events. It's dark-backgrounded to be visually distinct from the form, so it reads as reference, not input. Engineers can check what they need without going anywhere.

Priority uses a visual selector rather than a dropdown, because priority decisions under pressure shouldn't require reading through a list. The pre-submission checklist at the bottom of the sidebar catches the things engineers tend to miss before they hit submit.

Screen 5 of 5 — Confirmation
The last screen matters more than people think

The previous confirmation was a toast notification that disappeared after a few seconds. For engineers who'd just submitted a maintenance request for a turbine with vibration anomalies on a critical compression line, that wasn't reassuring.

This screen gives them a work order reference number, a full summary of what was submitted, who it was sent to, and what happens next in numbered steps. It's not decorative — it gives engineers confidence that the right information was captured and routed, and reduces follow-up calls to the service team because the expected timeline is already visible.

Handoff
Getting the prototype to build correctly

One thing I learned early on this project: a good prototype that doesn't survive handoff is just a good prototype. The gap between what was designed and what previous cycles had actually built was significant — because developers were being handed flat screens and making their own calls on validation logic, conditional fields, and error states.

For every screen I delivered an annotated spec alongside the Figma prototype. The spec covered every validation rule per field, what triggers it, the exact error message copy, what conditional logic shows or hides which fields, and how each component behaves in each state. I also ran handoff sessions with the dev team for the more complex forms — not to explain the design, but to answer the questions that would otherwise come back as bugs two weeks into build.

The maintenance request form alone had around 20 individual interaction rules between it and the confirmation screen. Documenting those wasn't extra work — it was how I made sure the design made it to production in one piece.

Reflection
What I'd do differently, and what I wouldn't

The thing I'd change: I had no success metrics going in. I made design decisions I believed in, and the feedback from the team was positive, but I can't tell you by how much error rates dropped or whether form completion improved. On future projects like this I'd define those measures upfront — even roughly — so the work can be evaluated against something real at the end.

What I wouldn't change: the time spent on handoff documentation. It felt slow, adding annotation layers and writing spec notes alongside the design. But it's what kept the build from diverging. And in a context where what gets built is what engineers actually use every day, that mattered.

The other thing I'd keep: writing contextual error messages. "Required" on every field would have been faster. But engineers who know their domain deeply need the system to be honest with them about what it needs and why. Generic messages fail that. Specific ones don't.

Requirements come from the system. The design has to come from the user.

Every requirement I was given was accurate from a data perspective. The problem was translating it into a flow that matched how a field engineer actually thinks during registration. That translation is where the design work lives.

The confirmation screen is part of the form, not an afterthought.

The form ends when the engineer is confident something happened — not when they click submit. A work order reference and a clear next-step timeline isn't optional in high-stakes workflows. It's the last part of the design.

Annotated handoff is the last mile of interaction design.

Developers building from well-documented specs don't have to make design decisions. Developers building from flat screens do. The difference shows up in production — not in review.

Inline validation only works if the messages are specific.

"This field is required" tells an engineer nothing they didn't already know. "Required for Class A compliance tracking" tells them why it matters and makes it harder to skip.
