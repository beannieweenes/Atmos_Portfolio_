Where it started
Pregnancy is overwhelming — and existing apps weren't helping

When we started looking into this space, one thing became clear quickly: there are dozens of pregnancy apps out there, and almost all of them do one or two things reasonably well and nothing else. You get the week-by-week baby development tracker, or the symptom logger, or the community forum. What you don't get is something that actually helps a pregnant woman manage the full reality of her day — her nutrition, her mental state, her appointments, her to-do list, her sleep.

Pregnancy is a physical experience but it's also an emotional and logistical one, especially for first-time mothers. The women we spoke to weren't looking for another article about folic acid. They wanted something that felt like it understood their situation and helped them get through the day. That gap — between what exists and what's actually needed — is where MOMents came from.

The brief was to design an AI-powered wellness app that brought personalized meal plans, mental health support, appointment scheduling, task management, sleep tracking, music therapy, and baby development tracking into one coherent experience. My job was to make that feel like a single, connected product — not a collection of features bolted together.

The problem with what's already out there
Fragmented tools for a moment that needs wholeness

We looked closely at What to Expect, Ovia Pregnancy, Flo, and Peanut. Each one has clear strengths — Flo's interface is clean, Peanut builds real community, What to Expect is trusted and content-rich. But none of them come close to addressing the full picture.

What to Expect doesn't touch mental health or nutrition in any meaningful way. Ovia is focused heavily on fertility rather than the ongoing pregnancy experience. Flo's pregnancy mode is a bolt-on to a cycle tracker, not a first-class feature. Peanut is about community, which is valuable, but it can't remind you to drink water or book a therapy session.

The gap we identified was specific: no app combines AI-personalised nutrition, physical wellbeing, mental health access, sleep tracking, and task management in a single experience. MOMents was designed to be that app — and designed from the start for the emotional register of pregnancy, not just its clinical needs.

Design Journey
How we got from research to finished screens
The project moved through five stages — from user interviews and competitor mapping through to wireframes and final hi-fi designs. Here's what that progression looked like.
1
Research & Problem Definition
User interviews · Competitor analysis · Problem framing

We began by speaking with expectant mothers across different stages of pregnancy — first-time mothers in their early twenties, experienced mothers managing a second pregnancy alongside an existing child, and OB-GYN doctors who could tell us what their patients were failing to do. The consistent theme: fragmentation. Every woman was using three or four different apps or resources, and none of them talked to each other.

The competitor audit confirmed the gap. Flo handles cycles but its pregnancy mode is a bolt-on. What to Expect is trusted content but zero personalisation. Peanut is community. Ovia is fertility-first. Nothing does it all — and nothing tries.

User Flows & Information Architecture
Mapping how a user moves through the app across all six feature areas

Before any screens, we mapped the complete information architecture. Six core sections — Home, Tasks, Wellbeing (Mental + Physical), Sleep, and Learning — connected by a persistent bottom navigation. The key decision here was treating Mental and Physical Wellbeing as tabs within a single section rather than separate nav items. They overlap constantly in pregnancy, and separating them would have fragmented the experience.

Visual Direction & Style Exploration
Finding the right tone — warm without being childish, clinical without being cold

The visual direction was the most deliberate decision in the project. Pregnancy apps almost universally go one of two ways: pastel cartoons (feels like it's designed for the baby, not the mother) or clinical white (feels like a hospital app). We went a third way — a deep, dark base that's comfortable at any time of day, with lavender and rose pink as warm, feminine accents that feel like the app understands who it's for.

Rejected: Pastel
Feels designed for babies, not mothers
Rejected: Clinical
Cold and impersonal for a deeply personal experience
Chosen: Warm Dark
Comfortable at 3am, distinctly feminine, grown-up
5
Hi-Fidelity Designs
Six fully designed screens ready for prototype and investor presentation

The hi-fi screens were designed in Poppins throughout, with a consistent dark base and the lavender/pink system applied across all six sections. Every screen was reviewed against the wireframe to confirm that the structural decisions survived into the visual design — and against the user research to confirm the feature set answered the needs we'd identified.

On the design decisions
Why it looks the way it does

The palette — lavender, rose pink, deep ink — was a deliberate choice to feel warm and feminine without being infantilising. A lot of pregnancy apps default to pastel pinks and cartoon storks. That design language signals "for babies," not "for the person having one." MOMents is designed for adult women going through one of the most demanding experiences of their lives, and the visual language needed to reflect that.

The dark background was also a deliberate call. Pregnancy disrupts sleep, and a lot of app usage happens at night. A bright white screen at 2am when you can't sleep is the last thing you want. The deep ink background is comfortable at any hour, and it makes the lavender and pink accents glow rather than shout.

Every screen was designed around the idea that the user might be tired, nauseous, anxious, or dealing with something urgent. That meant generous touch targets, short text, clear hierarchy, and nothing that requires reading a paragraph to understand. The AI features — personalised meal plans, music curation, task suggestions — are woven in quietly rather than announced as features.

What we learned
What I'd carry forward from this

Designing for pregnancy meant designing for a moving target. The user's needs in week 8 are completely different from week 32 — physically, emotionally, and informationally. That required building a system flexible enough to personalise across the entire journey, not just at onboarding.

The competitor analysis reinforced something important: feature completeness is not the same as a good product. Every app we looked at had features. None of them had coherence. The job wasn't to add another feature — it was to make all the features feel like they belonged to the same product, designed by someone who understood what pregnancy actually feels like.

If I was doing this again, I'd push harder on the AI personalisation earlier in the process. The AI meal planning and music curation are compelling, but they needed more definition around how the system learns and adapts week by week. That's the part that makes MOMents genuinely different from everything else — and it deserved more design attention than it got in the initial prototype.

Pregnancy isn't a problem to be solved. It's an experience to be supported. That's the difference between a feature set and a product that actually helps.

Design for the person, not the condition.

Pregnant women are adults managing complex lives. The design language needed to reflect that — sophisticated, warm, and clear — not cute or patronising.

Holistic means designed holistically, not just listed holistically.

It's easy to say "we cover nutrition, sleep, mental health, and tasks." Making those feel like one coherent experience instead of separate tabs is the actual design work.

Tired users need simple screens.

Short text. Big targets. Clear hierarchy. The person using this at 3am can't be expected to read a tooltip or figure out a novel interaction pattern.

The AI should be invisible until it's needed.

The value of AI personalisation isn't the label "AI-powered" — it's the moment a user opens the app and thinks "how did it know I needed this?" That's what we were designing toward.
