Where it started
Someone noticed doctors were spending half the appointment looking at a screen

This came out of Capgemini's corporate health clinic setup. The observation was pretty simple — go sit in a GP's room for a morning and watch what actually happens. The doctor asks a question, the patient answers, and instead of the doctor listening and thinking, they're typing. Then they're looking up what the patient was prescribed six months ago. Then they're searching for a drug interaction. Then they're trying to remember what the patient said three minutes ago while they were doing all of that.

By the time a ten-minute appointment is over, a good chunk of it has gone into documentation, not medicine. And the patient walks out with a prescription they half-understand and no real memory of what was said.

The idea behind this project was: what if the app just listened? Record the conversation, transcribe it in real time, pull out the clinical information — diagnoses, prescriptions, tests — and build the record automatically. The doctor talks to the patient. The app handles the paperwork.

I came in to design both sides of it — the interface the doctor uses during and after the consultation, and the app the patient opens on their phone afterwards.

The actual problem
Same data, completely different people trying to use it

The tricky part of this project was that there were really two separate design problems sitting on top of the same technical thing.

For the doctor, the biggest risk was actually the app itself. If you put a screen in front of a doctor during a consultation, they'll look at it. They can't help it. So the doctor-facing interface had to earn its place in the room without pulling attention away from the person sitting across the table. Everything needed to be readable at a glance. Nothing should require clicking around to find.

For the patient, the problem was different. Most people leave a doctor's appointment having taken in maybe half of what was said. They forget the dose. They can't remember if the blood test was for the thyroid thing or something else. They lose the printed prescription somewhere between the clinic and the pharmacy. The app needed to give them back that information in a form they could actually use — not the clinical record, but a plain-language version of it. And their medication reminders needed to set themselves, because nobody is going to sit down and configure a reminder schedule after a GP appointment.

There was a third problem too. When you visit a specialist who's never met you before, you become the only source of your own history. You have to remember what you were prescribed, when, by whom, and for what. Most people get bits of it wrong. With this app, a cardiologist could open a patient's record and see every GP visit, every diagnosis, every prescription — all there, without the patient having to narrate it from memory.

Screen 1 — Live Consultation
The screen that had to stay invisible

This one took the most iterations. The first version had too much going on — the transcript was front and centre, the AI suggestions had buttons on them, there were controls for pausing and rewinding. It looked comprehensive. It also looked like something a doctor would minimise immediately and never open again.

We stripped it back. The recording indicator is just a small red dot and a timer. One button: end the session. The transcript scrolls on its own. Anything clinical — drug names, test mentions, diagnoses — gets highlighted automatically so the doctor can scan without reading. The patient's allergy flags and active meds sit in a dark sidebar panel that's always there but never demands attention. The AI panel below it surfaces relevant notes based on what's being said in the room — not as actions to take, just as context.

The rule we kept coming back to was: if the doctor has to touch this screen during the consultation, something has gone wrong.

The decision behind it

We debated adding approve/reject buttons to the AI suggestions. It would have felt more interactive, more "in control." But every button you add is attention you're taking away from the patient in the chair. We left them read-only.

Screen 2 — Visit Summary
The doctor reviews it, not rewrites it

When the session ends, the app generates a structured clinical record from the transcript. Chief complaint, vitals, diagnoses with ICD-10 codes, prescriptions, tests ordered, follow-up notes. It doesn't look like a transcript dump — it looks like what a doctor would write if they'd had time to write it properly.

The review state was the hardest design problem here. Doctors need to trust what they're putting their name on. We couldn't just show the generated summary and assume they'd accept it. So there's a clear "Pending Review" state, obvious edit affordances throughout, and a confidence score from the transcription model — if the system was less sure about something, the doctor can see that and double-check.

Once they finalise it, the record becomes visible to the patient in their app and to any other doctor the patient has authorised. That transition — from draft to shared — felt like the right moment to make explicit.

Screen 3 — Patient History
Seven visits across three doctors, all in one place

This is the screen that solves the specialist problem. A cardiologist opening this before seeing a patient for the first time can see that they were first diagnosed with hypertension six months ago by their GP, were referred and evaluated, started on Amlodipine, had two follow-ups, and came in today with morning headaches and fatigue. That full story is visible without a phone call to the GP's office and without asking the patient to reconstruct it from memory.

The profile card on the left shows the things that need to be seen immediately — blood group, allergies, active conditions, who else is treating this patient. The timeline on the right is ordered by date, with the key clinical tags from each visit visible without opening anything. Overdue follow-ups, critical test results, referral outcomes — all surfaced at the card level.

The patient controls who sees this. In the patient app there's a named list of authorised clinicians with a one-tap way to remove access. That felt important to get right — this data is sensitive, and the patient should never be in a position where they can't tell who has access to their full history.

Screen 4 — Prescription
Generated from the conversation, verified by the doctor

When the doctor mentions a medication during the consultation — "let's increase your Amlodipine to 10mg" — the app picks that up from the transcript and populates the prescription automatically. Dose, route, frequency, duration. The doctor reviews and signs off.

Each drug card has the clinical detail at the top and plain-language patient instructions below — "take one tablet every morning, avoid grapefruit juice" rather than just the dosing notation. The allergy flag sits at the top of the prescription so it's visible to the pharmacist or any other clinician who opens the record.

The prescription goes to the patient as a QR code in their app. They can walk into any registered pharmacy and show that screen. No printed paper, no chance of losing it on the way home.

Screen 5 — Patient App
The same appointment, written for someone who isn't a doctor

This was genuinely the most interesting part of the project. The patient app uses the same underlying data as the clinical record, but it can't look or read anything like it.

Where the clinical record says "ICD-10: I10 — Essential hypertension, controlled, improving," the patient app says "your blood pressure is getting better." Where the clinical note says "Amlodipine dose titrated from 5mg to 10mg OD," the patient app says "your blood pressure tablet has been adjusted to a higher dose — take one every morning." That translation work — every term, every phrase — was done in collaboration with the clinical team to make sure simpler didn't mean inaccurate.

Medication reminders get created automatically from the prescription. The patient doesn't configure anything. They open the app the next morning and the reminder is already there at 8am. Lab results come through as a notification when they're ready, with a plain explanation of what to expect at the next appointment.

The nine screens below show the full patient journey: the home screen, visit summary in plain language, medication detail, health history, prescription with QR code, lab results with plain explanations, notifications, the doctor access consent screen, and the patient profile. Each screen is navigable from the tab row above the phone.

Patient App — plain-language visit summary, medication reminders, lab results, appointment tracking

The patient app isn't a simplified version of the clinical record. It's a completely different document that happens to come from the same source.

What was genuinely hard
Three things that took longer than expected

Getting the consultation screen passive enough was harder than it sounds. Every time we added something useful — a way to flag a transcript line, a button to accept an AI suggestion — it pulled focus. There were several rounds where we'd add a feature, look at it, and delete it. The restraint felt counterintuitive at first. Usually when you're designing something you're trying to add value. Here, removing things was the value.

Making the auto-generated summary trustworthy was a slow process. Early on, doctors reviewing the prototype would spot one small error in the generated record and lose confidence in the whole thing. We added confidence scoring, clearer editing affordances, and a section-by-section review flow. It took a few rounds to find the right balance between "the app has done the work for you" and "you're still in control of what goes in the record."

The consent flow — who sees what, and how patients control it — got squeezed at the end of the project timeline. It was designed, but it didn't get the same iteration time as the other screens. In hindsight that was the wrong call. In healthcare, consent isn't a secondary feature. If I were doing this again I'd start there, not finish there.

Looking back
What I'd do differently, and the part I'm glad we got right

The thing I'd keep: the decision to make the consultation screen almost entirely passive. There were multiple conversations where someone wanted to add interactivity to it — approve a suggestion, edit a transcript line mid-session. Every time, we pushed back. The consultation is between the doctor and the patient. The app is there to record it, not participate in it. That principle made the screen what it needed to be.

What I'd change: the patient notification strategy. The medication reminders worked well, but the rest of the app was still fairly passive — you open it to look things up. A more useful version would help patients arrive at their next appointment better prepared. What's changed since last time. What the doctor asked you to keep track of. What questions to bring up. That kind of active preparation would make the app useful between visits, not just after them.

I also didn't set up proper success metrics going in. The feedback from clinical staff was positive, but "positive feedback" isn't a measurement. I should have agreed on what good looked like before the prototype was built — something like: how long does it take the doctor to finalise a record? How many corrections do they make to the generated summary? How many patients open the app within 24 hours of their appointment? Those numbers would tell you whether the design was actually working.

Designing for two users means accepting that they want opposite things.

The doctor wants less to look at. The patient wants more explanation. Same data, completely different jobs. The mistake would have been trying to reconcile them into one interface.

In healthcare, the tool has to earn trust before it can help.

One wrong diagnosis code in the generated summary and a doctor stops trusting the whole system. The confidence scoring, the review state, the editing — those weren't nice-to-haves. They were the precondition for the tool being used at all.

Simpler language takes more work, not less.

"Hypertension — controlled" is four words. Writing "your blood pressure is in a better range now, which is good news for your heart over time" is harder, takes longer, and requires someone who understands both the medicine and the patient.

A screen that asks for nothing is still doing something.

The consultation interface required zero interaction during the session. That felt like a design failure at first — shouldn't a well-designed tool be useful to use? The answer here was no. Being present without demanding attention was the whole point.
