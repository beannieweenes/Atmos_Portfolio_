01 — The brief
Give doctors back the half of the appointment they lose to a keyboard

Capgemini ran an internal health clinic, and the brief came out of one observation: in a ten-minute consultation, the doctor spends half of it typing instead of listening. My job was to fix that — for the doctor, and for the patient on the other side of the desk.

02 — Discovery
I spent a morning watching real consultations before I drew anything

The pattern repeated every visit. The doctor asks a question, the patient answers, and instead of thinking the doctor is typing — then hunting for a past prescription, then checking a drug interaction, then trying to recall what was said two minutes ago while doing all of it.

By the end, a chunk of the appointment has gone to paperwork, and the patient walks out remembering maybe half of what happened. That handed me three problems to design for, not one.

03 — Who it was for
Same data, three very different people

The doctor. Put a screen in front of them mid-consultation and they'll look at it — they can't help it. The interface had to earn its place in the room without pulling their eyes off the patient.

The patient. They leave having absorbed half of what was said, lose the paper script, forget the dose. They needed the visit handed back in plain language, with medication reminders that set themselves — because nobody configures a reminder schedule after a GP appointment.

The next specialist. They meet the patient cold and rebuild the history from memory — usually getting bits of it wrong. They needed the whole record in front of them, without a phone call to the GP's office.

04 — Principles
Three rules I designed against

The consultation screen has to stay invisible. The patient app can't read like a medical record. And trust is the feature — a doctor won't sign their name to something they don't believe.

05 — Process
Most of the work was deciding what to take out

The live-consultation screen took the most rounds. The first version put the transcript front and centre, buttons on the AI suggestions, playback controls — comprehensive, and exactly the kind of thing a doctor minimises and never reopens. So we cut: the flag-a-line control, the accept-suggestion button, anything that asked for a tap. Every removal made it better. The rule we landed on — if the doctor has to touch this screen mid-consultation, something has gone wrong.

Trust took the longest. Doctors reviewing the auto-generated summary would catch a single wrong line and stop believing the whole thing. That's where the confidence scoring, the obvious edit affordances, and the section-by-section review came from — the line between "the app did the work for you" and "you're still in control of the record."

The patient app was rewritten line by line with the clinical team, so "your blood pressure is getting better" never drifted away from "I10 — essential hypertension, controlled."

06 — The solution
Five screens, one source of truth

The same record powers everything — captured passively in the room, reviewed by the doctor, then shown two ways: the clinical version for anyone treating the patient, and a plain-language version for the patient themselves. 07 — Validation
It went back to the people it was for

Doctors tested the prototypes. The sharpest signal was the summary-trust problem — one error collapsing confidence in the whole record — and the review flow exists because of how hard they pushed on it.

08 — Reflection
What I'd keep, and what I'd change

Keep: the passive consultation screen. Every push to make it interactive got resisted, and that restraint is what made it work — the consultation is between the doctor and the patient, and the app's job is to record it, not join in.

Change: consent got squeezed to the end of the timeline. In healthcare that belongs at the start, not as a finishing touch — if I did this again I'd design the "who sees what" flow first.

And I didn't set success metrics going in. "Positive feedback" isn't a measurement. Next time I'd agree on what good looks like before building — time to finalise a record, corrections per generated summary, patients opening the app within 24 hours of a visit.

Designing for two users means accepting they want opposite things.

The doctor wants less to look at; the patient wants more explained. Same data, opposite jobs. The trap would have been forcing them into one interface.

In healthcare, a tool earns trust before it gets to help.

One wrong code in a generated summary and a doctor is done with the whole system. Confidence scoring and the review state weren't polish — they were the price of being used at all.

Plain language is more work, not less.

"Hypertension, controlled" is two words. Saying it so a patient actually understands takes someone who knows both the medicine and the person reading it.

A screen that asks for nothing is still doing its job.

The consultation interface needed zero interaction during the session. Being present without demanding attention was the entire point.
